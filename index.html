Lutheran Congregational Singing

The hymnal we use to sing hymns in our churches employs Lutheran congregational singing, a genre of music typically written for four mixed voices. It was Martin Luther who made an effort to spread the news of the Reformation through singing in a universal language of the people, using well-known popular melodies and incorporating religious texts (Doukhan, 2017).

Not only did he revolutionize religion, but music also became a form of protest. Before 1517, the Catholic Church held control over music throughout most of Europe, with laypeople not participating and only listening to plainchants (single-voice chants) in Latin sung in choir (Valentino, 2017). Chorales underwent rhythmic changes and syncopated patterns until reaching their current form (Doukhan, 2017).

The harmony found in four-voice hymns is:

  • Originally composed of chords.
  • These chords are arranged or placed in different voices to balance the sound and provide an aesthetic beauty.
  • Generally, the soprano voice carries the melody
  • The other three voices accompany the melody, creating the sonic equilibrium.
  • On occasions, more than four voices are used.

Chords

A chord is a combination of three or more notes placed at a distance of a third interval in each voice (Tchaikovsky et al., 2014).

The triads primarily used in hymns are formed on each degree of the scale using only three notes. We can use Roman numerals corresponding to the scale degrees to identify the chords, using uppercase for major chords and lowercase for minors.

Also, they can be identified by the chord's root name. The only disadvantage of using this chord nomenclature is that it does not indicate the role it plays within the tonality. In this text, we'll use both forms of nomenclature to illustrate the examples provided.

Names of Chords

As chords are formed by two intervals of a third, they can create different combinations that result in four different types of chords: major, minor, diminished, or augmented. It's useful to remember that the smallest distance in the scale is the semitone, and a major third interval consists of four semitones, while a minor third is equivalent to three semitones.

Arrangement of a Triad

In choral writing, each voice should have its corresponding clef. However, to simplify and reduce costs, it's written using two staves, employing the treble clef for female voices and the bass clef for male voices.

When using triads in a four-voice set, one voice is duplicated, preferably the root, and secondly, the fifth of the chord is duplicated, avoiding the duplication of the third. Generally, the bass sings the chord's root, but occasionally, for melodic beauty, ornamental notes like passing tones or leaps are used. Hence, at times, the bass might sing the third or the fifth.

Four-note chords are constructed by adding a third to the fifth degree of a triad, and they're termed as seventh chords.

Chords to which the sixth degree is added to triads are called sixth chords. There are two types, using both the major and minor triad.

Harmonic functions

Each chord position serves a harmonic function, allowing for the naming of each chord according to the degree. Transitioning from one chord to another generates different sensations, tonal function can either be stable or create tension based on the harmonic context set by the composer in various parts of the composition. The I degree fulfills the tonic function, the IV degree serves as the subdominant, and the V degree is the dominant.

The tonic chord

The chord of the first degree is named the tonic chord because it serves as the tonal center, marking a point of rest or the center of gravity. This chord provides stability and is present as both the first and last chords in most hymns from the hymnal, with only a few exceptions.

Its function as a point of rest is crucial in Western tonal music as it establishes a sense of conclusion or resolution after different harmonic progressions.

The subdominant chord

This chord holds the value of the IV degree and creates a certain tension that leads to resolution either towards a higher tension (to the dominant) or towards stability (returning to the tonic). It's considered "subdominant" because it precedes the dominant chord, which is the V degree, acting as an intermediate step between the tonic chord's stability and the tension generated by the dominant chord.

Other degrees can fulfill the subdominant function.

The Dominant Chord

This chord contains a tritone (three whole tones) built on the fifth note of the scale, generating significant instability. Its function is to harmonically resolve towards a stable chord such as the I or VI degree to create a sense of rest or tonal conclusion. The tension generated by the fifth-degree chord is due to the presence of the minor seventh interval, occurring between the third note of the fifth-degree chord and the seventh note of the scale. This seventh tends to resolve to the scale's first note.

The II Degree

The second degree is called the supertonic, constructed on the second note of the diatonic scale and represented as a minor triad. In the key of C, it's composed of the notes D-F-A.

Its main function is to add a bit more variety and harmonic movement. It doesn't possess the same resolution strength as the tonic, subdominant, or dominant degrees, but it can be used to create interesting harmonic progressions and moments of tension that eventually resolve.
